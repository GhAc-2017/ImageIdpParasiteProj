Babesiosis

The genus Babesia is a  group of   organisms   which   infect   animal erythrocytes,    causing     a  disease      known  as       babesiosis  or prioplasmosis. The condition  is   distributed world widely  and affects many   host species,  particularly  cattle, sheep, goat and horses. Babesiosis is a seriously debilitating disease and is   frequently fatal. The  parasites are   passed from infected animals by the agengy of various species of ticks. Those animals which recover from the disease carry a residual infection in the  animal  population and   in the ticks.

Cattle  babesiosis,    caused by Babesia divergens,   can be a serious problem in    restricted  areas in  Britain. This  species  is one   of the group of 'small' Babesia which may be  distinguidhed from the other members by the widely  divergent arrengment  of the  dividing pairs which are  closely  applied to the periphery of  the erythrocytes. The tick which is responsible for its  transmission, Ixodes ricinus ,  has an abnormally long life cycle lasting about 3 years.  Parasites can be  seen most readily in blood  smears taken  from the  infected  animals at or just prior    to  the    peak   of  fever.    At   this   stage,  haemoglobinuria  commences and the animal become anaemic.

A large piroplasm, Babesia major has been detected in British cattle, associated with the tick haemaphysalis puncata which occurs in isolated habitats.

No vector of horse babesiosis, caused by B. equi and B. caballi,occur in the  British Islandes.  Many   countries  however, prohibit  the entry of horses carrying  residual infections.  These may  be detected by the Complement-fixation  test,    which is   carried  out  at    the   Central Veterinary Laboratory, Weybridge  in support  of the  UK bloodstock industry.

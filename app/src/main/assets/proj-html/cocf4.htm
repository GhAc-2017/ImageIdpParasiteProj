When an outbreak of coccidiosis is suspected it is essential to obtain an  adequate  history,   and for a  definative diagonosis  it is  necessary to examine post mortem material.  It must be  stressed that,  particularly in ruminants,   very  high  faecal oocyst  counts   (more than 107 per g)  can occur in healthy animals.   A diagonosis should be  based on the clinical history  and  examination of  post  mortem material.   Oocyst counts  can  help to support the diagnosis,  but it is importent  to identify the species. With most coccidia of  mammals the species can best  be identified from sporulated  oocysts.  In poultry however   the species  canot be  identified reliably from the oocysts. For these hosts the infection may be identified from the  nature  and location  of the lesions   and the  appearance  of the  developmental stages of the parasites in tissue smears. A scheme for the systematic examination to infected chicken intestine is given here.

     Estimation of the number of oocyst in faeces are made by the modified Mcaster method. This technique gives a rough indication  of the severity of   infection but it  is  difficult to intercept.  In general,  the   presence of oocysts in  the faeces has  little clinical   significance.  Very high counts maybe noted in thefaeces of apparently healthy animals,whilst a condition of disease may exist when very few oocysts can be demonstrated.  

    Counts of oocysts in deep  litter material may be made by  the  modified McMaster technique  but again,  interpretation  is somewhat  difficult.   If several large samples are taken  from around  drinkers, under brooders, etc., and a  detailed examaintion  of the  oocysts is made   to determine  the percentage    which  have    sporulated,   a  resonable   assessment    of  the parasitic  contamination of the  litter and the consequent risks of disease may be made.

    Histological   studies may   be of   value  in   establishing    the  type  and location  of   the lesions    within  the  tissues   and to  determine   whether schizonts or  gameocytes are the main cause of tissue damage.

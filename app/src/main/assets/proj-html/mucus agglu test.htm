Mucus agglutination test

Agglutions of T. foetus often may be detected in the mucus of infected animals. They are constantly present in pyometra cases in varying titres and other animals they may be detected often during the 4-5 days following oestrus. Stock cultures of trichomonads are required as a live antigen.

If the mucus has any blood on it, it is better to collect another sample (serum contains non-specific agglutinins)

Method
    1. Set a water bath at 560C (10 0C above the melting point of agar)
    2. Melt a flask of agar in boiling water.
    3. Dilute the mucus 1:5 with physiological saline and emulsify in griffiths tube.
    4. Four test tubes are needed per sample – two of each labelled 1:10 and 1:20.
    5. Into the 1:10 tubes pipette 2 ml of mucus and into 1:20 tubes 1 ml of mucus and 1 ml of saline.
    6. Two tubes are needed for controls into which are pipetted 2 ml of saline.
    7. Put all tubes into the water bath at 56 0C.
    8. Flame a sterile pipette (to prevent agar solidifying) and add 2ml of agar to each tube.
    9. The mixure are then poured into appropriately labelled petri dishes and allowed to cool.
    10. Make up antigens by adding trichomonads to 2:1 mixure of saline and 1% Glucose broth. Twenty-four cultures of Manley and Belfast strains are added slowly until there are approximately 10 trichomonads per microscope field (1 x 105 per ml).
    11. Add 1.5 ml antigen to each petri dish.
    12. Leave the dishes in 37 0C incubator for 11/2 hours – remove and leave covered on the bench for 11/2 hours.
    13. Examine under the microscope for agglutination.

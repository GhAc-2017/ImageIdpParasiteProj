An antigen, a foreign substance (recognized as nonself by immune system, may be protein, carbohydrate, nuceleic acid or their complexes) when invades a suitable host provokes the formation of anti-body specific to the antigen. Parts of the antigen molecule responsible for generation of antibody response are called antigenic determinants or epitopes. In case of carbohydrate antigens, an epitopes usually consist of 4 to 6 aminoacids or sugar residues.  Most of the antigen molecules have multiple antigenic determinants on them and hence are multivalent i.e. carry many reactive sites. The antigenic specificity of epitopes is

dependent on its three dimensional structure. All the antigenic determinants on a given antigen may or may not be the same.

            Antibodies (immunoglobulins) synthesized in response to antigenic challenge are bivalent i.e. contain two indendent antigen combining sites complementary to the respective antigenic determinants. Antibodies synthesized against single antigen in a humoral immune response are heterogenous in specificity and affinity and may include all immunoglobulin classes. The heterogenicity of antibodies is due to the triggering and activation of different B cell clones in response to several antigenic determinants. Antibody molecules of five known classes of immunoglobulins i.e. IgG, IgA, IgM, IgD and IgE differ in their structure of the constant portion of heavy chains.

INDIRECT HAEMAGGLUTINATION TEST

(Using SHEEP RBC)

Reagent Test Important Note Animation

The agglutination of a particulate antigen by its specific antibody is, classically, the sinplest way of estimating the quantity of antibody in a serum. Red blood cells have been found to be extremly convenient passive carriers of antigen. Coated cells become most sensitive and stable reagent for antibody detection. Some antigens particularly the polysaccharides can be effectively coated by only exposing red cells for a short time. Among the general methods available for coating antigen on red blood cells the most widely used method is the tanned cell technique. Treatment of the red blood cells with tannic acid increases the amount of most protein antigens subsequently adsorbed. The higher density of coated antigen greatly increases the sensitivity of the agglutination reaction. The test can also be used in a reverse manner where tanned red blood cells can also be coated with antibody t test the presence of antigen.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Reagents and materials :

  • Alsever's solution : Dextrose 2.05 gms; sodium citrate 0.80 gms; sodium chloride 0.42 gms, dissolve in 90 ml distilled water. Adjust pH to 6.1 with 10% citric acid make up the volume to 100 ml and autoclave the solution. Phosphate buffered saline (pH 7.2) Stock solution (PBS): Na2H Po4 (anhydrous) 36.0 g; KH2Po4 (anhydrous) 2.15 g; NaCl 36.0 g; dissolve in distilled water and make upto 1 liter. Store the buffer at 4 0C. Stoke buffer can be diluted 1:5 just before use.

  • Antigen solution : 

  • Tannic acid solution (5 mg in 50 ml PBS): a good reagent grade tannic acid should be used. It is preferable to make up the solution freshly each time.

  • Sheep red blood cells (SRBC) : Sheep blood stored at 4 0c after mixing with an equal volume of Alsever’s solution.

 

Normal rabbit serum: (NRS, used as stabilizer).

  • In activated serum by heating at 56 0C for 20 minutes (to destroy complement) and absorbed twice with a quarter of its volume of packed SRBC from the same batch as those being coated.

  • Centrifuge: Screw cap bottle –50 and -100 ml; pipettes; Pasteur pippettes; Measuring cylinders; graduated centrifuge tubes; and Haemagglutination perspex trays.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Test Procedures:

 

Preparation of cells:

    • Collect 20 ml of sheep blood in Alserver’s solution. Wash cells thrice with PBS, by centrifugation at 750 g (about 1000 rpm) for 10 minutes.

    • Prepare 5% of SRBC suspension by adding 50 ml of PBS to 3 ml of packed cell pellet. (Starting volumes of 3 ml rather than 2.5 ml of packed cells is used to allow loss of cells during many washing procedures).

    • Mix 10 ml of 5% SRBC suspension to 10 ml of tannic acid solution in duplicate. Mix well and incubate in water bath at 37 0C for 15 minutes. Remove unreacted tannic acid by washing cells twice with PBS and finally resuspend the cells in 10 ml PBS. One tube with tanned cell is kept aside to be used as control cells and for absorption purposes. To the other tube, after centrifugation add 10 ml of PBS containing 20 mg BSA. Incubate at 37 0C for 30 minutes with intermittant shaking. In this step aanned cells are coated with antigen (BSA).

    • Wash the cells thrice with PBS containing 1% NRS to remove free antigen.

    • Prepare 50 ml of 1% antigen coated SRBC suspension with PBS containing 1% NRS. Cells are now ready for use in the test.

 

Tritration of Antibody :

 

    • Inactivate test serum for 30 minutes at 56 0C. Mix 0.1 ml of test serum with 0.9 ml of un coated tanned 1% SRBC ( to remove heterophile agglutinins). Incubate at room temperature for 15 minutes with intermittent shaking. Centrifuge the suspension at 1000 rpm for 10 minutes to recover test sample. Supernatant is used as 1:10 diluted test sample.

    • Make doubling dilution of test sample to one row of haemaglutination tary.

    • Add 0.1 ml of test sample to the first and second wells. Mix content in second well and continue to make doubling dilution by taking out 0.1 ml from each well after mixing and adding to next well. Discard 0.1 ml solution from the last well.

    • Add 0.1 ml of antigen coated 1% SRBC to all the wells. Mix well gently with the help of pasture pipette.

    • Keep suitable controls by using 0.1 ml of 1% uncoated tanned cells with and without 1:10 diluted test sample.

    • Leave the trays overnight at room temperature to observe agglutination of sheep erythrocytes. The end point is taken as the highest dilution showing a smooth mat of agglutinated cells with a crenated rim or complete carpet of cells covering the buttom of wells. Negative result is indicated by closely packed button or small ring formed by non-agglutinated cells.

    • In a doubling dilution series, a gradient between these two appearances usually occurs over 3 to 4 wells. This is usually graded as +++ (an even carpet of cells), ++, +, ± and – (a negative button).

 

 

Important notes

    • In antisera with very high agglutination titres, a prozone phenomenon may obscure the results. The prozone phenomenon produces falsely negative agglutination reaction at higher concentrations of antibody as a result of poor lattice formation and steric hindrance caused by antibody excess. However, the use of standard serial dilution eliminates this difficulty. Since IgM antibody is about 50 times as efficient as IgG in agglutination, the presence of high amounts of IgM may markedly influenece the test results.

    • It is important to remove heterophile agglutinins (antibodies) from test ample and normal rabbit serum to avoid nonspecific agglutination of sheep erythrocytes. If the noncoated cells are agglutinated with the lowest dilution of the sample, further absorption should be made until natural agglutinins have been removed.

    • If the sheep blood is collecetd asceptically in autoclaved Alsever’s sloution with Aureomycin (5 mg in 100 ml Alsever’s sloution), they can be used for at least 2 weeks. Sheep erythrocytes can also be preserved by formalinization after tanning and coating stage. For doing this 8% of tanned coated SRBC in PBS is mixed with equal volume of 3% neutralized formaldehyde. Formaldehyde should be added drop by drop with gentle agitation. Suspension is mixed well and gently agitated at 370C for 18 hours. Cells washed five times and preserved as 10% cell suspension with thiomersal at 40C for many weeks.

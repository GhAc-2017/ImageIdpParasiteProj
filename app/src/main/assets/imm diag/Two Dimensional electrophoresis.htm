Two Dimensional electrophoresis (2 D Electrophoresis)

Reagent        Test procedure              Animation

Antigen – antibody crossed electrophoresis also known as two-dimensional immunoelectrophoresis is a powerful combination of electrophoresis and semi-quantitative immunoelectrophoresis. In this procedure diffrenet proteins are separated by electrophoresis in agar and then again subjected to electrophoresis at 900 C angle in agar gel containing antibodies to the complex mixture of proteins. Precipitin peaks appear and area under individual peaks gives an estimate of the concentration of that component in the antigen mixture.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Reagents and material

  • Barbitone buffer (0.3 M pH 8.6)
  • 1% agarose in Barbitone buffer
  • Bromophenol blue
  • Antigen
  • Antibody
  • Glass slide ( 8 X 9 cm)
  • Electrophoresis apparatus
  • Well cutter and razor blade

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Test Procedure

  • Pour 10 ml of agarose on glass slide kept on levelling board.
  • Punch out three wells ( 2mm diameter) at the bottom of gel ( 1.5 cm from the cathode end)
  • Fill the wells with 4 ul of antigen solution.
  • Perform electrophoresis by applying 12 volts/cm for about 60 minutes. Use Bromophenol blue as marker dye.
  • Cut the gels into strips with the help of razor blade between the wells and along the direction of the electrophoresis.
  • Lift the sample section of the gel with the aid of a long blade, and place it upside down near the cathode edge of the second glass slide (8 X 9 cm). Fill the rest of the slide with agarose gel containing 0.2 to 4 ml of antibody depending on titre of the antibody. Allow the gel to solidify.
  • Apply current (2.5-3 volts/cm) at right angles to the separated proteins for 22 hours.
  • After the run is complete, wash the plate extensively and stain with any of the conventional protein stains.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Results

  • With crossed immunoelectrophoresis individual peaks are formed in Rocket shape. The area under each rocket is propotional to the amount of antigen present, and the position of the rocket can be used for identification of the individual proteins. This method is also useful for quantitative measurement of a number of antigenically distinct serum proteins in a single procedure.

Precautions

  • The temperature commonly used for precipitaion reactions varies from 0 – 560 C. Ideal temperature for antigen-antibody precipitaion is 250 C. Velocity of formation of precipitate increases with increased temperature initially, however amount of Ag- Ab precipitate may be less at higher temperature.
  • In order to avoid electroendosomosis, a highly purified agar or agarose should be used.
  • The undesired heat production during electrophoresis can be reduced by lowering of ionic strength of the buffer in the gel and /or by circulating cold water under the plate or running the electrophoresis at low temperature. (40C)
  • Certain amount of electrolyte presence is necessary for the formation of precipitate.
  • Suitable pH range for immunoprecipitation is 6.5 – 8.2, pH beyond this range will reduce specific immunoprecipitation.
  • Antigens capable of diffusion through the gel only can be used. Higher the molecular weight of immunoreactants, slower will be the diffusion. Increasing concentration of the gel will lead to decrease in the rate of diffusion of immunoreactants. The diffusion of larger molecular weight substances can be enhanced by decreasing the comcentration of agar/ agarose.
